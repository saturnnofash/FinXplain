## 1. The Philippine Fintech Landscape

### 1.1 Financial Inclusion Context

The Philippines presents a compelling case study for financial technology intervention due to the persistence of large unbanked and underserved populations despite significant recent progress. According to the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP) 2021 Financial Inclusion Survey (FIS), formal financial account ownership among Filipino adults (aged 15 and above) rose to 56% in 2021, a dramatic increase from 29% in 2019 (BSP, 2022a; Philstar, 2022). Despite this improvement, 34.3 million Filipino adults — approximately 44% of the adult population — remained unbanked as of 2021 (Senate Economic Planning Office [SEPO], 2023).

The structure of account ownership further illuminates the digital opportunity. E-money account ownership quadrupled from 8% in 2019 to 36% in 2021, while bank account ownership rose from 12% to 23% over the same period (BSP, 2023a). This indicates that digital wallets, not traditional banks, drove the bulk of the inclusion gains. The World Bank's Global Findex Database 2021 corroborates this trend, showing the Philippines as one of the economies where mobile money and fintech platforms contributed most to post-pandemic financial inclusion (World Bank, 2022a).

Common barriers to formal account ownership persist: insufficient funds, lack of documentary requirements, distance from financial institutions, and lack of trust in formal providers (SEPO, 2023). Among those with outstanding loans, 57% relied on informal sources, while only 4% borrowed from banks (BSP, 2023a). The BSP's National Strategy for Financial Inclusion (NSFI) directly targets these gaps, positioning digital financial services as the primary mechanism for reaching the underserved (BSP, 2020a).

### 1.2 Digital Payments Growth

The BSP's Digital Payments Transformation Roadmap (DPTR) 2020–2023 set two headline targets: converting 50% of total retail payment volume to digital form and onboarding 70% of Filipino adults into the formal financial system (BSP, 2020b; Diokno, 2020). By 2023, the BSP reported that digital retail payments reached 52.8% of total monthly volume, surpassing the 50% target (BSP, 2023b).

Progress has continued to accelerate. The BSP's 2024 Report on the Status of Digital Payments reported that digital payments reached 57.4% of total monthly retail payment volume and 59.0% of total transaction value (BSP, 2024a). Merchant payments were the primary driver, representing 66.4% of total digital volume. Government disbursements (G2X) achieved a 97.2% digitalization rate (BSP, 2024a). The underlying infrastructure — InstaPay for real-time low-value transfers and PESONet for batch electronic fund transfers — has been critical to this growth (BSP, 2024a).

COVID-19 significantly accelerated adoption. The pandemic forced lockdowns that limited physical banking access, pushing millions of Filipinos to adopt e-wallets and mobile banking for the first time (Acclime Philippines, 2024; Milken Institute, 2023). This created a structural shift in behavior that persisted well beyond the emergency period.

### 1.3 Regulatory Environment

The BSP serves as the primary supervisor of all banks, e-money issuers, and digital banks in the Philippines. Its regulatory framework encompasses several key instruments relevant to the providers in this study:

**BSP Circular No. 1105, Series of 2020** established the framework for digital banks, defining them as institutions that offer financial products and services end-to-end through digital platforms with no physical branches. The circular mandates a minimum capitalization of PHP 1.0 billion and subjects digital banks to BSP standards on corporate governance, IT risk management, cybersecurity, consumer protection, and anti-money laundering (BSP, 2020c; ADB Law and Policy Reform, 2020). Six digital banks have been licensed under this framework: Maya Bank, GoTyme Bank, UnionDigital Bank, UNObank, Tonik Digital Bank, and Overseas Filipino Bank (Maya, 2024a; Wikipedia, 2026).

E-money issuers such as GCash operate under separate BSP regulations governing electronic money and payment systems. All providers in this study are supervised by the BSP, and all deposit products are insured by the Philippine Deposit Insurance Corporation (PDIC) up to PHP 1,000,000 per depositor (PDIC, n.d.).

Unit Investment Trust Funds (UITFs), as offered through GCash GInvest, are regulated by the BSP under the Manual of Regulations for Banks (MORB), Section 414. UITFs require BSP authority for each fund class, mandatory Client Suitability Assessments, and weekly publication of Net Asset Value per Unit (NAVPU). UITFs are explicitly not deposit products and are not covered by PDIC insurance (UITF.com.ph, n.d.; BSP, 2024b).

The Philippine Data Privacy Act of 2012 (Republic Act No. 10173) provides the overarching framework for personal data handling by fintech providers, mandating that all institutions processing financial data implement appropriate security measures and obtain informed consent.

### 1.4 Why These Three Providers Represent the Market

GCash, Maya, and BPI were selected because they collectively represent the three dominant categories of financial service providers accessible to ordinary Filipinos:

1. **Mass-market e-wallet**: GCash holds approximately 89% of the Philippine mobile wallet market share (Statista, 2023) and has over 94 million registered users (FinTech Magazine, 2025). It is the dominant digital financial platform in the country.

2. **Digital bank**: Maya Bank is one of only six BSP-licensed digital banks (BSP, 2020c) and is recognized as the leading digital bank by multiple industry bodies (The Asian Banker, 2025; The Digital Banker, 2025). It reached 5.4 million bank customers by end-2024 and became profitable in Q1 2025 (ABS-CBN News, 2025).

3. **Traditional universal bank**: BPI is the second-largest universal bank in the Philippines by total assets (PHP 3.63 trillion as of December 2025) and one of the oldest financial institutions in Southeast Asia, founded in 1851 (BSP, 2025a; BPI, 2024a).

Together, these providers cover the primary savings and investment options accessible to most Filipinos, spanning the full spectrum from unbanked e-wallet users to formally banked professionals.

## 2. Justification for GCash

### 2.1 Market Position and User Base

GCash, operated by Mynt (Globe Fintech Innovations, Inc.), is a joint venture primarily owned by Globe Telecom and Ant Group (an affiliate of Alibaba), with Ayala Corporation holding a 13% stake (Philstar, 2024). The company reached a $5 billion valuation following a capital injection from Mitsubishi UFJ Financial Group (MUFG) and Ayala in August 2024 (Kapronasia, 2025).

As of late 2025, GCash reported over 94 million registered users (FinTech Magazine, 2025; Philstar, 2024), representing approximately 80% of the Philippine population (ResearchGate, 2025). GCash maintains an active user base reported to be five times larger than its closest e-wallet competitor (STL Partners, 2023). Statista (2023) data shows GCash commands approximately 89% of the Philippine mobile wallet market share, with Maya at 5%.

The platform supports a network of approximately 5.8 million merchants and social sellers and over 640,000 cash-in/cash-out (CICO) outlets (FinTech Magazine, 2025). GCash operates as a BSP-licensed e-money issuer, placing it under direct regulatory oversight.

### 2.2 Relevance to Target Users

GCash's relevance to the FinXplain target population (Filipino adults aged 18–65 across Metro Manila, urban, and rural areas) is rooted in its positioning as a mass-market financial platform. Its zero-cost account creation, accessibility via basic smartphones, and integration with the Globe Telecom mobile network make it particularly relevant for lower-income, unbanked, and first-time savers.

GCash has been integrated into government disbursement programs, enabling direct-to-wallet transfers for social protection beneficiaries (Afable, 2024). The platform's widespread CICO network ensures that users in rural and underserved communities can access cash services even without nearby bank branches.

The BSP's 2021 FIS data showing that e-money account ownership quadrupled to 36% (BSP, 2022a) is largely attributable to GCash's rapid user acquisition during and after the COVID-19 pandemic.

## 3. Justification for Maya (Maya Bank)

### 3.1 Market Position and Regulatory Status

Maya Bank, Inc. holds one of only six BSP digital banking licenses granted under Circular No. 1105, Series of 2020 (BSP, 2020c; Maya, 2024a). The bank is a subsidiary of Voyager Innovations, which is part of the PLDT Group (The Asian Banker, 2025).

Maya (formerly PayMaya) rebranded and launched its digital banking operations in 2022. By end-2024, the bank reported 5.4 million bank customers, PHP 39 billion in deposit balances, and PHP 68 billion in loans disbursed during 2024 alone (The Asian Banker, 2025). By Q2 2025, Maya's customer base had grown to 8.2 million, with deposits reaching PHP 50 billion (Punto Central Luzon, 2025). Maya achieved profitability for both its digital bank and e-wallet units in Q1 2025, reaching this milestone in under three years of operation (ABS-CBN News, 2025; Maya Bank, 2024b).

Maya has been named the "Best Digital Bank in the Philippines" for three consecutive years by The Digital Banker (Punto Central Luzon, 2025), as well as by Asian Banking & Finance and Global Finance.

### 3.2 Relevance to Target Users

Maya Bank explicitly targets the underserved and "unhappily banked" populations. Seventy percent of Maya's customers reside in regional areas outside of Metro Manila, where the bank has experienced faster growth in both lending and savings compared to the capital (The Digital Banker, 2025). Over 50% of Maya's 2.1 million borrowers are first-time formal borrowers (Punto Central Luzon, 2025).

Maya's high-interest savings rates — significantly above those of traditional banks — make it particularly attractive to younger, digitally active Filipinos seeking better returns on their deposits without the friction of physical bank branches. The BSP digital bank license ensures that Maya deposits are PDIC-insured up to PHP 1,000,000 (Maya Bank, n.d.a).

### 3.3 Product: Maya Savings

**Product Details**:

**Justification**: Maya Savings' base rate of 3.0% p.a. is approximately 48 times higher than BPI Regular Savings (0.0625% p.a.), making it a compelling option for moderate-income users who want significantly better returns without exposure to investment risk. The zero minimum balance requirement removes the financial barrier faced by low-income users. Daily interest crediting provides a tangible, visible reward that reinforces positive savings behavior, aligning with behavioral economics principles on immediate feedback and reward salience (Thaler & Benartzi, 2004).

### 3.4 Product: Maya Personal Goals

**Product Details**:

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Interest Rate | 4% to 8% p.a. (tiered, April–June 2026) (Maya, 2026) |
| Maximum Goals | 5 separate goal accounts (Maya Bank, n.d.c) |
| Balance Cap (per goal) | PHP 100,000 (for boosted rates) (Maya, 2026) |
| Lock-in Period | None (Maya Bank, n.d.c) |
| Interest Crediting | Daily computation, monthly credit (Maya, 2026) |
| PDIC Insurance | Up to PHP 1,000,000 (Maya Bank, n.d.c) |

**Justification**: Goal-based savings products are supported by substantial behavioral finance research. Thaler and Benartzi's (2004) Save More Tomorrow program demonstrated that commitment mechanisms and goal framing significantly improve savings outcomes. Mental accounting theory posits that earmarking funds for specific purposes (e.g., emergency fund, education, travel) reduces the psychological fungibility of money, making individuals less likely to spend savings impulsively (Thaler, 1999). Maya Personal Goals operationalizes these principles by allowing users to create up to five named, purpose-specific savings accounts, each with its own balance and interest accrual. Research from J-PAL has shown that commitment savings products can meaningfully increase savings among low-income populations in the Philippines specifically (Ashraf et al., 2006). For Filipino users saving toward culturally significant goals such as education, home ownership, or emergency preparedness, goal-based products provide both the psychological scaffolding and the financial incentive structure to sustain savings behavior.

## 4. Justification for BPI (Bank of the Philippine Islands)

### 4.1 Market Position and Institutional Standing

BPI is the second-largest universal and commercial bank in the Philippines by total assets, with PHP 3.63 trillion as of December 2025 (BSP, 2025a). In 2024, BPI reported net income of PHP 62.05 billion, with a Return on Assets (ROA) of 1.98% and Return on Equity (ROE) of 15.07% (BPI, 2024a). S&P Global Ratings has affirmed BPI's 'BBB+' credit rating, citing its strong capital position, liquidity buffers, and dominant market presence (S&P Global, 2025).

Founded in 1851, BPI is one of the oldest financial institutions in Southeast Asia. It is publicly traded on the Philippine Stock Exchange (ticker: BPI) and is a subsidiary of Ayala Corporation, one of the Philippines' largest and most diversified conglomerates. BPI holds a BSP universal banking license and is a PDIC member institution.

BPI has invested significantly in digital transformation, offering a full-featured mobile banking app (BPI App) and online banking platform. Its merger with Robinsons Bank has further expanded its retail branch and ATM network nationwide.

### 4.2 Why a Traditional Universal Bank Was Included

Including a traditional universal bank alongside digital providers was an intentional design decision grounded in the study's target population and the current state of Philippine financial inclusion.

Despite the rapid growth of e-wallets, bank account ownership remained at 23% of Filipino adults in 2021 (BSP, 2023a). Many Filipinos, particularly older generations and those in formal employment, still prefer or require traditional bank accounts. Employers frequently mandate payroll accounts at established banks, creating a structural dependency on institutions like BPI.

Furthermore, the BSP's 2021 FIS data shows that while 36% of adults had e-money accounts, only a subset of these users also maintained traditional bank accounts (BSP, 2023a). The inclusion of BPI products (which require `has_bank_account = 1` in the recommendation model) ensures that the system can appropriately serve formally banked users, providing recommendations that acknowledge the continued relevance of traditional banking infrastructure.

From a research design perspective, including both digital-native and traditional providers enables the recommendation system to surface meaningful contrasts between product categories, enriching the explainability output with more diverse and informative counterfactual suggestions.

### 4.3 Why BPI Specifically (Not BDO, Metrobank, or Others)

BPI was selected over other large universal banks for several reasons:

1. **Digital transformation leadership**: Among the "Big 3" Philippine banks (BDO, BPI, Metrobank), BPI has been recognized for its aggressive digital transformation strategy, including early adoption of mobile banking, biometric authentication, and digital account opening. The BPI App and BPI Online platforms offer functionality comparable to digital-native competitors.

2. **Product diversity within a single institution**: BPI offers savings, goal-based digital savings (#SaveUp), and long-term time deposits (Plan Ahead) within a single ecosystem, allowing the study to capture the full range of traditional banking products without requiring multiple bank providers.

3. **Retail banking focus**: While BDO Unibank is the largest Philippine bank by assets, BPI maintains a stronger retail banking orientation with products specifically designed for individual savers and young professionals.

4. **Expanded reach**: BPI's merger with Robinsons Bank has significantly expanded its branch and ATM footprint, particularly in provincial retail centers.

5. **Digital savings integration**: BPI's #MySaveUp product is accessible through the GCash GSave marketplace, demonstrating cross-ecosystem integration between traditional and digital platforms.

### 5.2 Coverage of User Need Segments

The eight products collectively cover all major savings and investment need segments for Filipino users:

**Segment 1 — Zero-barrier entry (no minimum balance)**: GSave and Maya Savings both offer PHP 0 minimum balance requirements, serving unbanked and first-time savers who cannot meet traditional account opening thresholds. This aligns with the BSP's financial inclusion targets (BSP, 2020a).

**Segment 2 — Goal-based saving**: Maya Personal Goals and BPI #SaveUp provide structured, purpose-driven savings mechanisms. Behavioral finance research demonstrates that goal-based savings accounts with earmarking features improve savings outcomes, particularly among low-to-moderate income populations (Thaler & Benartzi, 2004; Ashraf et al., 2006). Maya Personal Goals offers up to 5 separate named accounts with elevated interest rates, while BPI #SaveUp provides a minimal-barrier digital savings vehicle within the traditional banking ecosystem.

**Segment 3 — Safe guaranteed returns (time deposits)**: Maya Time Deposit Plus and BPI Plan Ahead Time Deposit serve conservative users with medium-to-long-term horizons who prioritize capital preservation. Maya's digital time deposit offers shorter tenors (3–12 months) at competitive rates (up to 6.0%), while BPI's product offers a 5-year fixed tenor with the institutional backing of a 174-year-old universal bank.

**Segment 4 — Investment growth (variable returns)**: GCash GInvest (UITFs) is the sole investment product, targeting aggressive users with long-term horizons who seek market-linked returns. The PHP 50 minimum investment democratizes access to capital markets for ordinary Filipinos (The Creed Guy, 2021; Esquire PH, 2021).

**Segment 5 — Traditional banking safety**: BPI Regular Savings serves employed users who require or prefer a formal bank account, offering ATM access, payroll integration, and institutional trust at the cost of minimal yield.

### 5.3 Why Other Providers Were Not Included

**BDO Unibank**: BDO is the largest Philippine bank by assets. However, at the time of study design, BPI demonstrated stronger digital transformation leadership among traditional banks and offered a more cohesive range of retail savings products (savings, digital auto-save, and time deposit) within a single institution. Future iterations of FinXplain should consider incorporating BDO products to expand coverage.

**UnionBank / UBX**: UnionBank has strong digital capabilities and operates UnionDigital Bank. However, its retail savings product accessibility and brand recognition among the general population are lower compared to BPI. UnionBank's primary target market (tech-forward early adopters) overlaps significantly with Maya's user base.

**CIMB Bank Philippines (standalone)**: CIMB Bank Philippines is already represented in the system through the GCash GSave partnership. The standalone CIMB app has lower adoption compared to the GSave-embedded experience, making a separate inclusion redundant.

**Tonik Digital Bank**: Tonik holds a BSP digital bank license and offers competitive interest rates (up to 6.0% for time deposits) (FintechNews PH, 2026). However, its market penetration and brand recognition are lower than Maya's at the time of study. Tonik is recommended for inclusion in future work.

**SeaBank, OctoPay, and other neobanks**: These are emerging players with lower market share, less publicly documented product stability, and in some cases different licensing categories (e.g., rural banking licenses rather than digital banking licenses) (Wikipedia, 2026). Scope was limited to established, well-documented, BSP-regulated products.

This scope limitation is acknowledged as a stated constraint of the study. Future work should expand the provider set as more digital banks achieve market maturity and as product data becomes more publicly accessible.

## 6. Academic Defense of Provider Selection Criteria

### Criterion 1: Market Representativeness

The three selected providers collectively represent the three dominant categories of financial service providers accessible to ordinary Filipinos. GCash commands approximately 89% of the e-wallet market (Statista, 2023). Maya is the leading BSP-licensed digital bank by customer count and has been named Best Digital Bank for three consecutive years (Punto Central Luzon, 2025). BPI is the second-largest universal bank by total assets (BSP, 2025a). This three-provider framework captures the structural diversity of the Philippine financial services market — a principle consistent with financial system diversity arguments in development economics (World Bank, 2022a).

### Criterion 2: Financial Inclusion Coverage

The providers span the full spectrum of financially included and underserved users. GCash and Maya serve the unbanked and underbanked through zero-minimum-balance accounts accessible via smartphone. BPI serves the formally banked population requiring traditional banking infrastructure. The BSP's 2021 FIS confirms that 36% of adults had e-money accounts while 23% had bank accounts (BSP, 2023a), indicating that these provider categories collectively cover the majority of the financially active Philippine population.

### Criterion 3: Product Type Diversity

The eight products span all major savings and investment product categories recognized in Philippine personal finance: pure liquid savings (GSave, Maya Savings, BPI Regular Savings), goal-based savings (Maya Personal Goals, BPI #SaveUp), time deposits (Maya Time Deposit Plus, BPI Plan Ahead), and investment products (GCash GInvest UITFs). This diversity ensures the recommendation system can serve users across the full risk-tolerance and investment-horizon spectrum, from conservative short-term savers to aggressive long-term investors.

### Criterion 4: Regulatory Compliance and Verifiability

All three providers are regulated by the BSP and/or SEC. GCash operates as a BSP-licensed e-money issuer. Maya holds a BSP digital bank license under Circular 1105. BPI holds a BSP universal banking license. All deposit products are PDIC-insured. All product details are documented in official provider sources. This ensures the study is grounded in real, verifiable, legally compliant financial products — not hypothetical constructs.

### Criterion 5: Data Availability for Synthetic Generation

Sufficient publicly available data exists on user demographics, product eligibility criteria, interest rates, minimum balances, and product features for each provider to construct realistic synthetic data generation rules. GCash, Maya, and BPI all publish product details on official websites and help centers, and are covered extensively by Philippine financial media (MoneyMax, BusinessWorld, Philstar). This practical research constraint favors well-documented, publicly accessible providers.

### Criterion 6: Relevance to Study Population

The target population of this study is Filipino adults aged 18–65 across Metro Manila, urban, and rural areas. GCash (94 million registered users), Maya (8.2 million bank customers, with 70% outside Metro Manila), and BPI (second-largest bank with nationwide branch coverage) are collectively among the most recognizable financial brands among this population (FinTech Magazine, 2025; Punto Central Luzon, 2025; BSP, 2025a).

## 7. Anticipated Panel Questions and Responses

### Q1: "Why did you not include BDO, which is the largest bank in the Philippines by assets?"

BDO is indeed the largest Philippine bank by total assets. However, BPI was selected because it offers a more cohesive set of retail savings products (regular savings, digital auto-save, and long-term time deposit) within a single institution, and it has demonstrated stronger digital transformation in its retail offerings. BPI's integration with the GCash ecosystem through the #MySaveUp product also demonstrates cross-platform relevance. Including BDO is recommended for future iterations of this system to expand traditional banking coverage (BSP, 2025a).

### Q2: "Why did you choose only 3 providers? Is that enough to be representative?"

Three providers were selected to cover the three dominant structural categories of Philippine financial services: mass-market e-wallet (GCash, 89% market share), digital bank (Maya, BSP-licensed leader), and traditional universal bank (BPI, second-largest by assets) (Statista, 2023; BSP, 2025a). With eight distinct products spanning liquid savings, goal-based savings, time deposits, and investments, the system covers all major product categories accessible to ordinary Filipinos. The scope was deliberately bounded to ensure that all products could be thoroughly documented and validated with publicly verifiable data.

### Q3: "GCash GInvest involves investment risk — is it appropriate to recommend investment products to potentially low-literacy users?"

GInvest UITFs are regulated by the BSP under MORB Section 414, which mandates Client Suitability Assessments before any investment (BSP, 2024b; UITF.com.ph, n.d.). The FinXplain system recommends GInvest only for users with aggressive risk tolerance and long-term investment horizons, and the explainability layer explicitly communicates the risk factors contributing to the recommendation. The system's counterfactual explanations also show users what changes would shift them toward lower-risk alternatives, providing informed decision support rather than unsupervised product promotion.

### Q4: "How do you ensure the products you included are still current and accurate? Interest rates change frequently."

All product details were verified against official provider sources (GCash Help Center, Maya Bank official website, BPI official website) at the time of writing (April 2026). The system architecture separates product metadata (stored in `product_info.json`) from the recommendation logic, allowing product details to be updated without retraining the model. The documentation explicitly notes the verification date for all rates and acknowledges that interest rates are subject to provider change.

### Q5: "Why did you not include insurance products or credit products in your recommendation scope?"

The study scope was deliberately limited to savings and investment products for two reasons. First, savings products have simpler eligibility criteria that can be modeled from demographic and financial profile data alone, without requiring credit history or health information. Second, the BSP's financial inclusion strategy prioritizes savings account ownership as the entry point for formal financial participation (BSP, 2020a). Insurance and credit products represent valid future research extensions.

### Q6: "Maya Bank is relatively new — how reliable is it as a study subject compared to a 174-year-old institution like BPI?"

Maya Bank holds one of only six BSP digital banking licenses, requiring a minimum capitalization of PHP 1.0 billion (BSP, 2020c). It achieved profitability in under three years — faster than any other BSP-licensed digital bank (ABS-CBN News, 2025). Its deposits are PDIC-insured up to PHP 1,000,000, providing the same statutory protection as BPI deposits. Including Maya alongside BPI is precisely the analytical value of the study: it enables comparison between traditional and digital banking products under identical regulatory safeguards.

### Q7: "Is it ethical to use synthetic data to train a model that recommends financial products to real people?"

The use of synthetic data is a deliberate research design choice. Synthetic data generation follows parametric distributions calibrated to publicly available demographic and economic statistics from the Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA), BSP, and World Bank. This approach avoids the privacy risks and consent complications of collecting real financial data from individuals. The synthetic data and its generation methodology are fully documented and reproducible. The model's recommendations are accompanied by explainable AI outputs (feature contributions, plain-language reasons, and counterfactual suggestions) that enable users to understand and critically evaluate each recommendation before acting on it.

### Q8: "How did you determine the eligibility rules for each product in your label generation? Are they accurate?"

Product eligibility rules were derived from official product documentation published by each provider (e.g., minimum balance requirements, account types required, risk classifications). Where exact eligibility criteria were not publicly documented, rules were inferred from product descriptions and explicitly noted as "inferred from implementation" in the synthetic data documentation. The label generation logic is open-source and fully auditable in the project's `data/generate_synthetic_data.py` script.
